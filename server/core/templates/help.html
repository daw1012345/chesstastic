How to play Chess

Chesstastic follows the exact same game rules as a normal game of chess, but if you’ve never played chess before or would like a refresh of the rules you’re in the right place!

Setup

Let’s start with the setup. Chess is played on a board with 8 columns and 8 rows, so 64 squares. The squares alternate in color between white and black (or in our case green), as you can see in the pictures. We will call them ‘black squares’ or ‘white squares’ when referring to them. These squares form the playing field where you and your opponent battle it out. You and your opponents both have pieces that are located on these squares. There are 8 types of pieces: pawn ♟︎, rook ♜, knight ♞, bishop ♝, queen ♛ and king ♚. Each piece is either black or white of color, which, depending on which color you’re playing as, determines which pieces are your own and which are your opponents. When you start a game of Chesstastic, the game randomly selects which side you will be playing as. In the pictures to follow, we will assume you’re playing as white, but since the same rules apply to both black and white, it doesn’t matter which side you’ll actually be playing as. The game

In a game of chess, both players alternate in taking turns. The player with the white pieces always has the first turn. A turn consists of making one move on the chessboard. In a move, a player takes one of their own pieces and moves it to a different square, where it is allowed to move to. This can either be an empty square or a square with an opponents piece on it, but never a square that already has one of the players own pieces on it. We will talk more about which movements a piece can make later on. A player may never move a piece of the opponent and only one move can be made per turn. There is one special move in which more than one piece is moved in one turn, but this is explained in detail later on.

Movements

Now you know what the pieces on the board look like, but how do they actually move? Each type of piece has his own set of movements and it’s very important to know exactly which ones are available at which time. We’ll start with a simple one and move up from there.

Rook
Each player starts with two rooks in the corners of their side of the board. The rook can only move in horizontal or vertical lines, it cannot jump over other pieces and it can take any opponents piece that it can move to. When a piece is taken, it is removed from the board. Bishop
Each player starts with two bishops on their side of the board. The bishop moves very similar to the rook, it can only move in diagonal lines, it cannot jump over other pieces and it can take any opponents piece where it can move to. In practice this means a bishop can never change the color of the square it stands on. What does this look like on a chessboard?

Knight
Each player starts with two knights on their side of the board. The knight has an odd way of moving. It can either move two squares vertically and one square horizontally or one square vertically and two squares horizontally. It is the only piece that can jump over other pieces and just like the rook and the bishop, it can take any opponents piece where it can move to. What does this look like on the board?

Queen
Each player starts with only on queen on their side of the board. (notice how we said ‘starts’). The queen is the most powerful piece in the game, it can move vertically, horizontally and diagonally! It cannot jump over other pieces and it can take any opponents piece where it can move to. This means you can easily use the queen to put a lot of pressure on the opponent, but be careful: losing your queen can lose you the game fairly easy as well. What does the movement of the queen look like on a chessboard?

King
The king is the most important piece on the board, it must be protected at all times, since when you lose it, you lost the game. The movement set of the king is quite limited: it can move in all directions, but only one square far. It can take any opponents piece that it can move to but it cannot jump over other pieces. What does this look like on the board?

Next to these basic moves, the king does have two special moves it can make. They are both forms of castling. Castling is the only move that allows two pieces to move during the same turn. During castling a king moves two spaces towards the rook that it will castle with, and the rook jumps to the other side. The king can castle to either side as long as all of the following are true:

  1. The king has not moved.
  2. The king is not threatened by any opponents pieces.
  3. The king does not move through or into squares that are threatened by any opponents pieces.
  4. There are no pieces between the king and castling-side rook.
  5. The castling-side rook has not moved.

Pawn
The final, and most common piece on the board is the pawn. Each player gets an entire row of pawns, that’s eight of them. A pawn can move only one square in forwards direction (up for white, down for black). You might wonder why we left the pawn until the end since its basic move is the simplest of all. This is the case because the pawn actually has quite some special moves which it can only perform under certain circumstances.

  • Attack move
    Let’s start with the attack move, a pawn cannot take pieces by moving forwards horizontally, it will be blocked by any other piece. However, it can take pieces diagonally. Only when an opponents piece is located either diagonally left or diagonally right one square away from the pawn, can the pawn perform an attack move. In this move, the pawn takes the opponents piece and moves to the place where the opponents piece was.
  • Double Jump™
    Another special move can make is a double move. Only when a pawn has not moved from its starting row, can the double move be performed. When a pawn performs a double move, it moves two squares forward instead of one. A double move cannot jump over other pieces and is also blocked when other pieces are on the destination square.
  • En Passant
    The third special move of the pawn is called ‘en passant’. This move can only be performed when an opponents pawn has just performed a double move and is moved to a square next to the pawn. In that case, the pawn can move diagonally in the direction of the opponents pawn as if performing an attack move. The opponents pawn is taken during this move. En passant is the only move in chess where the piece that takes is not moved to the location of the taken piece.
  • Promotion
    The final special move a pawn can make is called a promotion. When a pawn reaches the second to last row of the chessboard (for white the seventh row, for black the second row) it can perform a promotion. The pawn performs a regular move, either moving forward or attack, and is moved to the final row. The pawn can now be replaced by a knight, bishop, rook or queen, which is why the move is called a promotion. The player can decide for themselves which piece the pawn should be replaced by. There are no limits as to how many knights, bishops, rooks or queens a player can have at a time, so theoretically, a player could have nine queens when all pawns are promoted.

Check and Checkmate

When a king is under immediate attack, it is said to be in check. A move in response to a check is legal only if it results in a position where the king is no longer in check. This can involve capturing the checking piece, interposing a piece between the checking piece and the or moving the king to a square where it is not under attack. As we showed before, castling is not a permissible response to a check.

So how do you win a game of chess? The object of the game is to checkmate the opponent. This occurs when the opponent's king is in check, and there is no legal way to get it out of check. It is never legal for a player to make a move that puts or leaves the player's own king in check. This means that when the opponent is checkmated, you won the game!

End of the game

There are multiple ways a game of chess can end. The most definite one is winning by checkmate. We already explained how to checkmate your opponent, when this happens, the game ends and the winner is the player who checkmated their opponent. Another way to win is to have the other player resign the game.

A game can also end in a draw. A draw can occur when a single game state occurs 3 times in a match. This can happen after 3 consecutive moves, but doesn’t have to be the case. A player can also always offer a draw, if their opponent chooses to except, the game ends in a draw.
